The start line of your first ultra will not look like what you expect. There's no gun, no corrals, no spectator barricades. It's more like a large group of people in running vests standing in the woods before sunrise. Here's what to actually expect.

## The Night Before: What to Do

Pack everything the night before. Lay it all out on the floor: shoes, socks, vest loaded with nutrition, bib pinned to your vest, watch charged, headlamp charged (if required), warm layer for the start if it's cold.

Eat your normal pre-long-run dinner. Set two alarms. The most common race morning disaster is oversleeping — set your backup alarm 10 minutes after your primary.

## Race Morning Timeline

**3 hours before start:** Eat your pre-race breakfast. The same thing you've eaten before your long training runs. 400–600 calories.

**2 hours before start:** Leave for the race venue. Ultra races are often at trailheads in remote locations with limited parking. The drive takes longer than expected, parking is further from the start than expected, and there is almost always a line for the bathrooms.

**60 minutes before start:** Check in, pick up your bib if you haven't pre-registered, and walk the start area. Find the bathrooms and note the line length. Use them now and plan to use them again 20 minutes before start.

**30 minutes before start:** Fill your flasks, apply sunscreen and anti-chafe, confirm your watch is recording and GPS is locked. Move near the start area but don't stress about position. Ultra starts are self-seeded and casual.

**10 minutes before start:** Use the bathroom again. This is not a joke — the combination of adrenaline and pre-race coffee creates urgency.

**At the start:** The race director will give safety instructions, course reminders, and any last-minute changes. Listen carefully. Then the race starts — usually with a simple countdown, not a gun.

## What the First Miles Feel Like

The first mile of your first ultra will feel too easy. The adrenaline is high, the crowd energy is positive, and the trail is fresh. Every instinct will tell you to run faster.

Run slower. The runners you see surging past you at mile 2 are the ones you will pass at mile 20 when they're walking in pain. Start 30–60 seconds per mile slower than your planned easy pace. This is not weakness — it is strategy.

## Aid Stations: What to Do

Your first aid station will be somewhere between miles 4 and 8. Pull off to the side of the trail, not in the middle of traffic. Grab what you need quickly. Drink, eat something, refill one flask if you're low. Thank the volunteers — they're giving up their weekend for you.

Don't sit down. Sitting at an early aid station is a psychological trap — it signals to your body that you're done, and getting up feels harder than it should.

## The Ultra Running Community on Course

Ultra running is one of the most welcoming athletic communities in the world. Runners chat. Experienced runners offer encouragement. The back of the pack gets the same cheers as the front.

If you're having a hard time, say so to a fellow runner. You'll likely get a gel, a joke, and a story about their worst race. This community is genuinely different from road racing.

Your first ultra will be harder than you expect and more rewarding than you imagine. Trust your training, start slow, and enjoy every mile. See our [race day checklist](/blog/race-day-checklist) to make sure you have everything packed.
