Beginners fear uphills. Experienced ultra runners fear the downhills. Descending incorrectly causes the deep quad soreness that turns the final 10 miles of a hilly ultra into a hobbling disaster. Here's how to descend properly.

## Why Do Downhills Destroy Untrained Legs?

Downhill running is dominated by **eccentric muscle contractions** — your quads are lengthening under load to slow your descent. This type of contraction causes more muscle damage than the concentric contractions of uphill running. Runners who haven't specifically trained for downhills arrive at their first mountain 50K and cannot walk the next day.

The muscle soreness isn't just uncomfortable. On race day, trashed quads in the final miles cause you to slow dramatically, alter your gait, and significantly increase your fall risk on technical terrain.

## What Is the Correct Downhill Running Technique?

**Lean forward, not back.** This is the most important correction. Most beginners instinctively lean back and try to brake with each step. This loads the quads maximally and creates the braking force that trashes your legs. Lean slightly forward from the ankles — like you're chasing the hill — and let momentum work for you.

**Shorten your stride and increase cadence.** Shorter, quicker steps on a descent mean less time in the air and less impact force per step. Think of your feet lightly pattering down the hill rather than slamming into it with long strides.

**Keep your knees slightly bent on landing.** A soft knee absorbs impact. A straight-leg landing transmits full force to the joint.

**Look further down the trail.** Your eyes should be scanning 10–15 feet ahead, not directly below your feet. This lets you plan footfalls in advance and maintain flow.

**Use your arms.** Arms out to the side like airplane wings increases stability on technical descents. Let them swing freely for balance.

## How to Train for Downhills

Training for descents requires two things: practice running downhill, and strength to support it.

**Practice:** Find a 300–500 foot descent near your home. Run it repeatedly — 4 to 6 repeats — once per week during your training block. Start controlled and gradually increase pace as your legs adapt. Your quads will be sore after the first session. This is normal and is exactly the adaptation you're building.

**Strength work:** The most effective exercises for downhill capacity:

- **Slow eccentric single-leg squats:** Lower for 4 seconds on one leg. This directly builds the braking strength used on descents. 3 sets of 8 per leg.
- **Step-downs:** Stand on a step, lower your non-working foot slowly past the edge. 3 sets of 10 per leg.
- **Wall sits:** 3 x 60 seconds builds the sustained quad endurance that long descents demand.

## The Right Shoes for Downhill Running

Shoe choice matters on descents. You need enough outsole grip to prevent slipping, a protective toe cap to absorb impacts on rocks, and enough lateral stability to handle side-angled footfalls on off-camber terrain.

The [Salomon Speedcross 6](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Salomon+Speedcross+6&tag=finishultra-20) is better on muddy descents where grip is the primary concern.

## When to Walk Downhills

Walking steep or technical descents is often the smartest race strategy, not a sign of weakness. Elite ultra runners walk sections that aren't efficiently runnable. The calculation is simple: if running a downhill costs more energy and risk than walking it, walk.

Keep a brisk hiking pace on descents you're walking — 3.5–4 mph is achievable even on steep terrain. This keeps your time loss minimal while preserving your quads for the runnable miles ahead.
