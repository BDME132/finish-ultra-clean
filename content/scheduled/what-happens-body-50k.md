Understanding what is happening inside your body during a 50K helps you make better decisions about pacing, fueling, and when to push versus when to back off. Here's what's actually going on, phase by phase.

## Miles 0–5: The Setup Phase

Your body enters the race with full glycogen stores (assuming proper pre-race nutrition), elevated adrenaline, and fresh muscles. Heart rate and core temperature rise rapidly in the first mile.

**What's happening:** Blood flow shifts toward working muscles. Digestive system blood flow reduces. Your body begins its primary fuel mix: approximately 50% glycogen, 50% fat oxidation at easy ultra pace.

**What this means for you:** This is the phase where most runners go too fast. Adrenaline masks effort. The easy pace you ran in training feels like a jog. Slow down. The effort that feels too easy right now is correct.

## Miles 5–15: The Aerobic Window

This is the most efficient phase of the race. Your body is in its aerobic groove, fuel systems are functioning optimally, and cumulative fatigue hasn't yet begun compounding.

**What's happening:** Glycogen usage continues steadily. At your current rate of depletion, you have roughly 90–120 minutes of glycogen available at easy pace — which means without consistent fueling, you'll hit depletion around miles 15–20.

**What this means for you:** Start fueling at mile 5, not when you feel hungry. Your glycogen depletion has already begun. Taking in 200–300 calories per hour from this point extends your reserves significantly.

## Miles 15–22: The First Challenge

Glycogen stores are becoming meaningfully depleted even with fueling (you can only absorb ~60–90g of carbohydrate per hour, which partially offsets depletion but doesn't fully replace it). Muscle microtrauma from trail impact begins accumulating. This is when the race starts testing you.

**What's happening:** Your body increases fat oxidation to compensate for glycogen depletion. Fat oxidation provides energy but at a lower rate — this is the physiological basis of the "bonk" when glycogen runs low and fat oxidation cannot keep pace with demand.

Core temperature has been building for hours and thermoregulatory effort is increasingly costly.

**What this means for you:** Increase caloric intake if you've been under-fueling. Switch to liquid calories or easily digestible food as your gut becomes more sensitive. Slow down if necessary — the goal is sustainable effort, not maintaining a pace your fuel system can no longer support.

## Miles 22–31: The Final Push

Glycogen is significantly depleted. Your brain — which runs almost exclusively on glucose — is beginning to feel the effects of lower blood sugar. Muscular fatigue is real. The quads are deeply sore from cumulative impact. Perceived effort at the same pace has increased noticeably.

**What's happening:** Your body is relying heavily on fat oxidation plus whatever carbohydrate you're consuming. Central nervous system fatigue compounds peripheral muscle fatigue. Decision-making and pain tolerance both decline with sustained low blood glucose and cortisol elevation.

Flavor preference has shifted toward savory — sweet gels may start causing nausea.

**What this means for you:** Eat real food at late aid stations. Broth, potatoes, pretzels, flat Coke. Add caffeine if you've been managing it strategically. Shorten your mental horizon — think only about the next mile. The physical reserves to finish exist. Managing the mental equation is now the primary task.

## After the Finish Line

Post-race cortisol elevation suppresses immune function for 24–72 hours. Muscle glycogen replenishment takes 24–48 hours with adequate carbohydrate intake. Connective tissue and muscle microtrauma repair takes 2–4 weeks. Your immune system is vulnerable — sleep more and avoid sick contacts this week.
