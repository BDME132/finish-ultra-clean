Most ultra runners train exclusively in daylight and then encounter their first night section on race day. That's backwards. Night running is a skill, and like any skill, it needs practice before you need it.

## Does Your Race Have a Night Section?

Before diving in: check your race start time and expected finish time. A 50K starting at 7am with a 10-hour cutoff means potential darkness starting around 5pm. In October or November, sunset can come much earlier.

Use this rule of thumb: if your expected finish time is within 2 hours of sunset, you should train at night at least a few times before race day. If you're racing a 50-miler or longer, night running is mandatory training.

## What Headlamp Do You Need for Night Running?

Your headlamp is your most important piece of night running gear. Do not use a phone flashlight or a cycling light — neither illuminates enough of the trail periphery to run safely.

**Minimum spec:** 200+ lumens, adjustable brightness, top-head mount

**Best entry-level pick — Petzl Actik Core ($60):** 350-lumen maximum output, rechargeable via USB, weighs only 55g. This is what most ultra runners start with, and many never upgrade.

## How to Start Training at Night

Start simple. Take one of your easy runs — not your long run — and move it to after dark. Run a route you know well. Use the same trail you've covered dozens of times in daylight. Your first goal is just to experience how familiar terrain looks different under a headlamp beam.

**Week 1:** One easy 45-minute run on a known trail
**Week 2:** Extend to 60–75 minutes on the same trail
**Week 3:** Move to new terrain, still under 90 minutes
**Week 4+:** Add one night run to your long run week — run the final hour of your long run as darkness falls

By your peak training weeks, aim for at least two night sessions of 1+ hour on trail terrain similar to your race.

## How Does Running at Night Feel Different?

Night running changes your perception in several ways you don't expect until you experience them:

**Pace feels harder.** Your depth perception is reduced by the headlamp cone. Your brain works harder to process terrain, which increases perceived effort at the same actual pace. Don't fight this — slow down.

**Technical terrain becomes harder.** Roots and rocks are harder to read in direct headlamp light than in diffuse daylight. Step landing becomes less precise. This is a skill that improves with practice.

**Your eyes adapt slowly.** When you reach into a bright aid station tent, your night vision resets. Give yourself 2–3 minutes after leaving the aid station to re-adapt. Don't rush into fast terrain right after a brightly lit stop.

**Time distorts.** After 10pm, one hour feels like three. This is normal. Break the night section into segments and focus on the next landmark rather than the total distance remaining.

## What to Wear for Night Running in Training

Your regular running gear plus:

- **Headlamp** (mounted on your head, not your vest)
- **Reflective vest or strips** for any section on or near roads
- **Extra layer** — temperatures drop significantly at night, especially on exposed ridges
- **Phone** — charged, emergency contacts accessible

For trail-only routes, reflective gear is less critical. For any road crossing or shared-use path, wear something reflective. Drivers do not see runners in the dark.

## Race Day Night Section Strategy

By race day, you should have run in the dark enough times that it feels normal — just slower and more focused. A few specific race night tips:

**Check headlamp batteries the day before race.** A fresh charge for a rechargeable lamp, or new batteries for a disposable one. A dimming headlamp mid-race is fixable; a dead one is not.

**Slow down on descents.** Technical downhills are the highest-risk section at night. Slow to a walk if needed. A fall that injures you ends your race faster than any time penalty from caution.

**Embrace the silence.** Night sections in ultras are often the most peaceful part of a race. The crowds are gone, the temperature is cool, and you're moving through the dark in a way very few people ever experience. Many runners say it's their favorite part.
