You've trained for months. Race week arrives. Then the email lands: your ultra has been canceled. Here's how to handle it without unraveling your fitness or your mindset.

## Why Do Ultra Marathons Get Canceled?

Ultra marathons get canceled most often for **weather and natural events** — wildfire smoke, flooding, extreme heat, or ice — followed by **permit issues** with the Forest Service, BLM, or state parks, and occasionally **low enrollment** for smaller regional events.

Weather cancellations are increasingly common. Races in fire-prone regions now face late-season smoke windows. Coastal events deal with hurricane-adjacent weather. This is a reality of trail running that every ultra runner will eventually experience.

## What Happens to Your Entry Fee?

When the race director cancels, most offer one of three options:

**Full refund** — most common for catastrophic cancellations (fire, flood, force majeure)
**Deferral** — your entry rolls to the same race next year. You keep your training investment, just delayed.
**Transfer to another event** — race series like USATF Trail or regional organizations sometimes let you shift to a different race in the calendar year.

If you cancel — not the race director — the policy depends on the event. Most keep 50–100% of your entry fee unless you bought **race insurance** through platforms like Allianz or the registration service (EventBrite, RunSignup, UltraSignup). For any race over $150 or requiring travel, race insurance is worth the $15–$30.

## What Should You Do with Your Training?

Do not stop training. This is the most important piece of advice in this post.

Your fitness has a peak. After your longest training runs, you have roughly 4–6 weeks of prime race-readiness before your body starts to decondition. Use that window.

**Immediate steps:**
1. Check UltraSignup.com for races within 4–8 weeks in your region
2. Email the RD of a target replacement race — some hold spots for displaced runners from canceled events
3. Continue your current training load for 1–2 weeks while you find a replacement
4. If no replacement race is available, drop volume by 20% per week to maintain base fitness

## How to Handle the Mental Side of a Cancellation

Race cancellations hit harder than most runners expect. You've structured months around a specific date. Your crew has cleared their schedules. Your taper-week nerves have been building. And then it's just... gone.

Give yourself 24 hours to be genuinely disappointed. Then reframe.

Everything you built in training exists. The miles are in your legs. The nutrition practice, the mental training, the long run suffering — none of it disappears because a race was canceled. You are fitter than you were 16 weeks ago, and that fitness transfers directly to your next race.

## What If the Cancellation Happens on Race Morning?

This is the worst scenario — you've driven to the start line, and the RD pulls the race due to a sudden weather event.

**Don't run the course without authorization.** Beyond the legal issues, course markings may be pulled, aid stations won't be staffed, and you'll have no medical support if something goes wrong.

**Document everything.** Take a photo of the official cancellation notice. Keep all your receipts. You'll need them for insurance claims.

**Connect with other runners.** Most ultra communities have a same-day informal "alternative run" somewhere accessible. Check the race's Facebook group or local running club.

## How to Pick a Backup Race Before You Even Need One

The best time to identify a backup race is when you register for your primary race. Spend 10 minutes on UltraSignup to find one or two alternatives in your target window. You probably won't need them. But when your email inbox explodes at 5am race week, you'll be glad you have a plan.
