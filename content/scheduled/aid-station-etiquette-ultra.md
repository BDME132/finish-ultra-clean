Aid stations in ultras are nothing like water stations in road races. They're staffed communities where experienced volunteers keep runners moving, fed, and sane. Knowing how to use them correctly can save you 10+ minutes over a 50K — and awkward fumbling at a busy station is more stressful than it needs to be.

## How to Approach an Aid Station

Run into the aid station area, then step to the side of the main flow. Don't stop in the middle of the path where other runners are trying to pass. Most aid stations have a clear in-flow and out-flow — follow the direction other runners are moving.

Announce what you need as you arrive: "Can I get a refill?" or "Do you have any broth?" Volunteers are often handling multiple runners and a clear request gets you served faster than hovering silently.

## What to Do at Early Aid Stations (Miles 5–15)

Keep these stops short — 2–3 minutes maximum. Grab what you need from this list:

**Water/fuel refill:** Hand your flask to a volunteer or hold it under the spigot yourself. Fill one or both flasks depending on the distance to the next station.

**Eat something:** Grab 2–3 things. A banana, some pretzels, a couple of boiled potatoes. Eat while you refill flasks.

**Don't sit down.** Sitting at an early aid station is psychologically seductive and practically unnecessary. Your legs don't need rest at mile 8 — your brain is just using the chair as an excuse to slow down.

## What to Do at Later Aid Stations (Miles 20+)

These stations are where races can be saved or lost. At mile 20+ with drop bags or crew access:

**Check your feet first if something hurts.** A blister dealt with at mile 22 is a 5-minute stop. A blister ignored until mile 28 can end your race. Volunteers will help you — ask.

**Eat real food.** Later in the race, your body starts craving salt and real nutrition. Broth, potatoes, grilled cheese bites, and flat Coke become more appealing than gels. Eat what sounds good.

**Change socks if your feet are wet.** A fresh dry pair of socks at a late aid station feels like a reset button. Takes 3 minutes, saves your feet for the final miles.

**Don't stay too long.** The longer you sit at a late-race aid station, the harder it is to leave. Set a mental timer — "I'll be moving again in 5 minutes" — and stick to it.

## The Unwritten Rules

**Thank the volunteers every time.** Every single time. These people woke up before you did, will be there after you finish, and are making your race possible. A genuine thank-you matters.

**Don't take more than you need.** Aid stations have limited supplies, especially at remote or smaller events. Take what you need for the next section. Don't load your vest with 10 cookies.

**Call out "Runner!" when passing someone at the entry.** Give other runners space. The aid station flow gets congested — be aware of who's around you.

**Don't block the table.** Fill your flask, grab your food, and step back. Other runners are behind you.

**Check in with the timing mat.** Some races require you to check in at each aid station for safety tracking. Look for the mat or ask a volunteer.

## When You're Having a Rough Patch at an Aid Station

Never quit at an aid station mid-crisis. This is the single most important ultra running rule. Instead: sit down, eat something, drink something, have a volunteer check your feet. Then walk out of the aid station, continue for 10 minutes, and reassess. Most bad patches pass within 15–20 minutes of movement and calories. The decision to DNF should never be made while sitting in a chair at mile 23.
