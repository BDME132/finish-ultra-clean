Commercial nutrition products have dominated ultra running for years. They're convenient and consistent, but they're also expensive, often overly sweet, and not always the best option for your gut over many hours. Real food has fueled humans through sustained effort for millennia. Here's how to make it work for your long runs.

## The Principles of DIY Ultra Nutrition

Any homemade ultra nutrition needs to deliver: **simple carbohydrates** (fast energy, easy to digest), **sodium** (electrolyte replacement), **sufficient calories** (200–300 per hour), and **portability** (survives in your vest pocket for hours).

Complex, high-fiber, or high-fat foods slow digestion at running intensity. Keep homemade options simple.

## Recipe 1: Date and Almond Butter Gel

The closest homemade equivalent to a commercial gel.

**Ingredients:** 4 Medjool dates (pitted), 1 tablespoon almond butter, 1/8 teaspoon salt, 2–3 tablespoons water

**Instructions:** Blend all ingredients until smooth, adding water gradually until you reach a thick gel consistency. Store in reusable soft gel flasks ($5–$8 at running stores or Amazon).

**Nutrition per serving (2 tablespoons):** ~120 calories, 25g carbohydrates, 3g fat, 200mg sodium
**Shelf life:** 3 days refrigerated

## Recipe 2: Rice Cakes (Ultra Running Standard)

Popularized by professional ultra runners, rice cakes are the most calorie-dense, palatable, and stomach-friendly homemade food for long runs.

**Ingredients:** 2 cups white rice (cooked, sticky), 2 tablespoons peanut butter, 2 tablespoons honey, 1/4 teaspoon salt

**Instructions:** Press cooked rice into a flat pan to 1-inch depth while warm. Mix in peanut butter, honey, and salt. Refrigerate 2 hours, cut into 2-inch squares, wrap individually in plastic wrap.

**Nutrition per cake (2 squares):** ~180 calories, 35g carbohydrates, 4g fat
**Shelf life:** 3 days refrigerated, 4–5 hours in vest

## Recipe 3: Homemade Electrolyte Drink

Covers sodium and potassium at a fraction of commercial product cost.

**Ingredients:** 24oz water, 1/4 teaspoon table salt (600mg sodium), 1/4 teaspoon NoSalt brand potassium chloride (780mg potassium), juice of half lemon, 1 teaspoon honey or maple syrup

**Instructions:** Mix and store in your soft flask. Costs less than $0.20 per serving versus $1–$1.50 for commercial alternatives.

Note: This covers electrolytes but not calories. Pair with solid food or a caloric gel.

## Recipe 4: Banana Energy Bites

Portable, natural, and widely tolerated even at late-race hour 6.

**Ingredients:** 2 ripe bananas, 1.5 cups rolled oats, 1/4 cup peanut butter, 2 tablespoons honey, 1/4 teaspoon salt

**Instructions:** Mash bananas, mix all ingredients, roll into 1-inch balls, refrigerate 1 hour.

**Nutrition per 2 balls:** ~120 calories, 22g carbohydrates
**Shelf life:** 4 days refrigerated, works for 3+ hours in vest

## Recipe 5: Potato and Salt Packets

The boiled potato with salt is the most trusted real food at ultra aid stations. Pre-prepare for your vest.

**Ingredients:** 2 medium potatoes, sea salt

**Instructions:** Boil potatoes, cube into bite-size pieces, toss with generous salt (500mg per serving), pack in a small silicone bag.

**Nutrition per serving:** ~100 calories, 22g carbohydrates, ~600mg sodium
**Best used:** Miles 20+ when gels become unpalatable

## Recipe 6: Medjool Date Packets

The simplest possible real-food gel alternative.

**Instructions:** Pit 6–8 Medjool dates, pack in a small bag. Eat 2 dates every 30–45 minutes. Each date provides 66 calories and natural sugars that digest easily.

Cost per serving: ~$0.50 versus $3–$5 for a commercial gel.

## Recipe 7: Salted Caramel Homemade Chews

**Ingredients:** 1 cup brown rice syrup, 1/4 cup coconut oil, 1 teaspoon vanilla, 1/2 teaspoon sea salt

**Instructions:** Heat brown rice syrup and coconut oil over medium heat until thick. Pour into a parchment-lined pan, refrigerate until firm, cut into 1-inch squares, wrap individually.

**Nutrition per 2 chews:** ~100 calories, 24g carbohydrates

## How to Transition to Real Food Nutrition

Start by replacing one commercial gel per long run with a homemade option. Test different recipes until you find 2–3 that your stomach handles well at running intensity. By the time you race, you should know exactly which options work for your gut and which don't.

Commercial products remain useful for their consistency and convenience on race day. Mixing commercial and homemade — as most experienced ultra runners do — gives you the best of both.
