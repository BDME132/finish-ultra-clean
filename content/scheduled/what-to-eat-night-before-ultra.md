The night-before meal is the most overthought meal in ultra running. Most runners either eat nothing substantial (out of pre-race nerves) or eat a massive pasta dinner they've never tried before (out of a misguided sense of obligation). Both are mistakes.

## What Is the Goal of the Night-Before Meal?

The goal is simple: top up glycogen stores, avoid GI stress, and eat something that lets you sleep. That's it. This meal will not make you faster. It can make you slower if you choose wrong.

## What Should You Actually Eat?

Eat **moderate-carbohydrate, low-fiber, low-fat food** that you've eaten before long training runs.

**Reliable choices:**
- White rice with grilled chicken or fish
- Pasta with plain tomato sauce (not cream sauce, not heavy meat sauce)
- A baked potato with olive oil and salt
- Bread with peanut butter and banana
- Rice bowls with simple protein

The common thread: simple carbohydrates, easy-to-digest protein, minimal fat, minimal fiber. These digest quickly and are fully processed before your race start.

## What to Avoid

**High-fiber vegetables:** Broccoli, beans, lentils, and raw salads increase gas and GI motility. Not what you want on the start line.

**Anything new:** This is the single most important rule. Do not eat a food you haven't had before a long run. Race night is not the time to try a new restaurant, a new cuisine, or a new meal prep.

**Alcohol:** Even one drink disrupts sleep quality and dehydrates you. Save the celebration for after the finish line.

**Excessive fat:** High-fat meals slow gastric emptying. A heavy, greasy meal sits in your stomach for hours and can cause nausea during the first miles of your race.

**Oversized portions:** Your glycogen stores are filled over the previous 48 hours of eating, not one meal. A massive pasta dinner the night before a race mostly causes bloating, poor sleep, and a sloshing feeling at mile 3.

## The 2-3 Day Carb Load (Better Than One Big Meal)

Real carb loading happens over 2–3 days, not one night. In the 48–72 hours before your race, increase your carbohydrate intake by 20–30% while reducing fat, fiber, and training volume.

Practical version: add an extra serving of rice or pasta to your meals, eat slightly more frequently, and cut your raw vegetable and high-fiber intake. You don't need to obsessively calculate grams — just shift the proportion of your plate toward carbs.

## Race Morning Eating

Eat your pre-race breakfast 2–3 hours before the start. What to eat: the same meal you've eaten before your long training runs. Most runners do well with oatmeal, toast with nut butter, or a bagel with a banana. 400–600 calories is a reasonable target.

Avoid eating less than 60 minutes before race start — your body won't finish digesting and the insulin response can cause an energy dip right as you're starting.
