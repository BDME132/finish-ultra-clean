Every post-race report from first-time ultra runners contains some version of the same sentence: "I went out too fast and paid for it at mile 20." Here's how to avoid being that person.

## Why Pacing a 50K Is Different from a Marathon

In a road marathon, experienced runners can hold a relatively consistent pace per mile because the terrain is predictable. Trail ultra pacing is fundamentally different — your pace per mile will vary by 5+ minutes depending on whether you're climbing, descending, or crossing technical terrain.

This is why **effort-based pacing** works better than pace-per-mile in trail running. Your target is a consistent perceived effort, not a number on your watch.

## How to Calculate Your Starting Effort

A useful framework: **your first 10 miles should feel embarrassingly easy.**

If you have a heart rate monitor, target **Zone 2** for the first third of the race. A rough estimate of your Zone 2 upper limit is 180 minus your age. At this effort, you should be able to speak in full sentences.

If you don't use heart rate, use the **conversation test**: if you can't talk comfortably, you're going too hard.

For a first 50K on moderate terrain, a reasonable rough pace target for your flat/runnable miles is **your easy training pace or slightly slower**. Don't try to run your tempo pace even for a single mile in the first half.

## The Run-Walk Strategy

Walking is not failure in an ultra. It is strategy. The most effective approach for most first-time 50K runners:

**Walk all uphills.** Even if you can run the hill, hiking it at 15–18 min/mile instead of running it at 13 min/mile costs you almost nothing in time while saving significant quad and cardiovascular effort.

**Run all flats and gentle descents.** Once the terrain flattens, pick up to your easy running pace.

**Walk technical sections.** Navigating roots, rocks, and tricky footing at a controlled walk is often the same speed as running nervously through them — and eliminates fall risk.

## A Simple 50K Pacing Framework

Divide the race into thirds:

**First third (miles 1–10): Hold back.** Run slower than feels right. Eat and drink on schedule. Talk to other runners. This is your investment phase.

**Middle third (miles 10–21): Maintain.** You should feel like you have more to give. That's correct. Hold back. Your effort will naturally rise as fatigue accumulates — don't add to it voluntarily.

**Final third (miles 21–31): Spend it.** This is when your conservative start pays off. While runners who went out hard are walking, you have the reserves to maintain pace or even pick it up.

## Estimating Your Finish Time

A rough calculation for moderate terrain 50Ks:

| Your easy training pace | Estimated 50K finish |
|---|---|
| 9–10 min/mile | 5:30 – 6:30 |
| 10–12 min/mile | 6:30 – 7:30 |
| 12–14 min/mile | 7:30 – 8:30 |
| 14–16 min/mile | 8:30 – 10:00 |

Add 30–60 minutes per 2,000 feet of elevation gain. Add time for aid station stops (budget 3–5 minutes per major station). Look at past results on UltraSignup for your specific race to calibrate against real data.

## What to Do When You Want to Speed Up

At mile 15, you will feel great and be tempted to surge. Don't. At mile 15 you have 16 miles left — more than half your race. The surge will feel good for 20 minutes and cost you significantly in the final third.

The mantra used by experienced ultra runners: **if it feels too easy, it's probably about right.** The race starts at mile 20.
