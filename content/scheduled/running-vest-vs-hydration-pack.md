The gear question every beginner ultra runner asks eventually: running vest or hydration pack? The answer affects how comfortable your race is, how easily you can access nutrition, and how much your gear moves during 6+ hours of running.

## What Is a Running Vest?

A running vest (also called a race vest or soft flask vest) sits against your chest and torso like a fitted garment. Water is carried in soft flasks in front pockets — typically two 500ml flasks at chest height. Storage is distributed across front and back pockets. The fit is snug to minimize movement.

## What Is a Hydration Pack?

A hydration pack (also called a hydration bladder pack) is a traditional backpack with a water bladder in the main compartment and a drinking tube routed over your shoulder. Most hikers and casual trail runners use hydration packs. They typically carry more volume than a running vest.

## Why Most Ultra Runners Prefer Vests

**No bounce.** The biggest problem with hydration packs during running is bladder movement. A partially filled bladder sloshes with each stride, creating distracting movement and hot spots on your back after a few hours. Well-fitted running vests with soft flasks essentially don't move.

**Easier water monitoring.** With front-mounted flasks, you can see how much water you have at a glance. With a bladder, you have no idea how full it is until you hear air in the tube.

**Faster aid station refills.** Pulling a soft flask from a chest pocket, holding it under a spigot, and replacing it takes 20 seconds. Removing a bladder, refilling it, resealing it, and replacing it takes 2+ minutes.

**Better nutrition access.** Front vest pockets are within reach without removing the vest. A hydration pack requires you to stop and dig through a back pocket.

## When a Hydration Pack Makes Sense

Hydration packs are better when: you're hiking more than running, you need to carry more than 2 liters of water between sources, or your race takes place in a remote area with sections longer than 15 miles between aid stations.

For most beginner 50Ks with regular aid stations, a hydration pack is unnecessary and inferior for running.

## The Best Running Vests for Ultra Runners

**Best overall — [Salomon ADV Skin 5](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Salomon+ADV+Skin+5&tag=finishultra-20) ($130)**
The most popular vest in ultra running. 5L capacity, extremely form-fitting, front pockets hold two 500ml flasks with easy access, stretch mesh pockets handle nutrition without bouncing. The gold standard for beginners.

**Best for hot weather — [Nathan VaporAir 3.0](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Nathan+VaporAir+3.0&tag=finishultra-20) ($150)**
Open mesh back panel provides dramatically better ventilation than enclosed vests. Worth considering if you run in consistent heat or sweat heavily.

**Best for 100-milers (more storage) — [Ultimate Direction Ultra Vest 6.0](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Ultimate+Direction+Ultra+Vest+6.0&tag=finishultra-20) ($160)**
10+ liters of storage for longer races where you carry extra layers, headlamp, emergency gear, and more food between remote aid stations.

**Most minimal — [Naked Running Band](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Naked+Running+Band&tag=finishultra-20) ($55)**
Just two front pouches for flasks and nutrition. No back panel, nearly weightless. Only viable for shorter ultras with very frequent aid stations.

## The Bottom Line

For your first 50K: buy a running vest, not a hydration pack. The Salomon ADV Skin 5 is the right choice for most body types and race conditions. If you already own a hydration pack and want to test it first, run a 15+ mile training run with it fully loaded. By the end, you'll understand exactly why most ultra runners switch.
