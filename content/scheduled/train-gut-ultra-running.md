Survey after survey of ultra runners who DNF reveals the same top reason: stomach problems. Not fitness. Not weather. Gut failure. The good news is that GI issues are almost entirely preventable with the right training.

## Why Does Your Gut Fail During Long Runs?

During sustained exercise, blood flow redirects from your digestive system to your working muscles. Your gut gets a reduced blood supply, which means it processes food more slowly. Add mechanical jostling, dehydration, and concentrated sugars, and you have the recipe for nausea, cramping, and a death march to the nearest bush.

The gut is also a trainable system. Runners who consistently eat during training runs have far fewer GI problems on race day than runners who skip nutrition during training and then try to fuel properly for the first time at their 50K.

## How Do You Start Training Your Gut?

Start on every long run over 60 minutes. Not just your 20-milers — every run over an hour. The adaptation happens through repeated exposure.

**Week 1–2:** Carry [Tailwind Endurance Fuel](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Tailwind+Endurance+Fuel&tag=finishultra-20) in one soft flask. Sip every 20 minutes. This is liquid calories with electrolytes — the easiest thing you can ask your gut to process while running.

**Week 3–4:** Add one solid food item per long run. A banana at mile 8. A [Spring Energy gel](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Spring+Energy+Awesome+Sauce&tag=finishultra-20) at mile 10. Practice chewing and swallowing at running pace.

**Week 5–8:** Work up to eating something substantial every 45 minutes on your long runs. This is exactly what race day demands — eating on a schedule whether you feel hungry or not.

## What Foods Train the Gut Best?

Train with foods you will use on race day. This is not the time for experimentation.

**Best for gut training:**
- Tailwind or similar liquid calories (simplest adaptation)
- Bananas and dates (easy to digest, portable)
- Real-food gels like Spring Energy (less concentrated sugar than traditional gels)
- Boiled potatoes with salt (used by experienced ultra runners everywhere)
- PB&J cut into quarters (fat + carbs + protein)

**Foods that frequently cause problems:**
- High-concentration gels used back-to-back without water
- Dairy-based products during hard efforts
- High-fiber foods close to long run start
- Anything you haven't practiced with before

## The Most Common Gut Training Mistakes

**Eating too much at once.** Your gut during exercise handles small, frequent inputs better than large sporadic ones. 150–200 calories every 45 minutes beats 600 calories at mile 15.

**Only hydrating with water.** Plain water without electrolytes can dilute your blood sodium and cause nausea, bloating, and in extreme cases hyponatremia. Always pair hydration with electrolytes on runs over 90 minutes.

**Skipping nutrition on training runs.** If you only fuel during races, your gut never adapts. The long run is your laboratory.

**Starting too late.** Start fueling at mile 5, not when you feel hungry. Hunger during a run means your blood sugar is already dropping. By the time you feel depleted, it takes 20–30 minutes to recover even if you eat immediately.

## Race Day Gut Strategy

By race day, eating while running should feel automatic. You should know exactly which products your stomach handles, at what intervals, and what to do when something isn't sitting right.

When your stomach goes sideways mid-race — and at some point it will — switch to broth, flat Coke, or plain water for 30 minutes. Give your gut a rest from concentrated nutrition, then reintroduce liquid calories gradually. Most GI issues during ultras are self-resolving if you catch them early and give your system a brief reset.
