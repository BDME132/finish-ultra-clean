Training hard for an ultra is supposed to make you tired. The question is: are you tired from productive training stress, or are you running your body into a hole? Here's how to tell the difference.

## Normal Fatigue vs. Overtraining: The Distinction

**Normal training fatigue** is acute. You feel tired after a hard week. After 1–2 easy days, you bounce back and feel ready to train again. Performance is maintained or improving on fresh legs. Resting heart rate is normal.

**Overtraining syndrome** is chronic. You feel tired despite rest days. A week of easy running doesn't help — you feel worse or the same. Performance is declining. Your resting heart rate is elevated. Sleep quality is poor despite exhaustion. You're getting sick more often.

The critical difference: normal fatigue resolves with rest. Overtraining does not resolve with short rest — it requires weeks to months of significantly reduced training load.

## The Warning Signs Specific to Ultra Runners

Ultra runners face specific overtraining risk because training volumes are high, the population tends toward obsessive consistency, and life stress compounds training stress in ways that are hard to perceive in the moment.

**Elevated resting heart rate.** Check your resting heart rate first thing in the morning before getting up. A consistent elevation of 5+ beats per minute above your baseline is a significant warning sign. Most GPS watches track this automatically. Set a 30-day baseline, then watch for sustained elevation.

**Declining performance despite training.** If your easy runs feel harder at the same pace, or your long run pace slows consistently without an obvious explanation (heat, hills, fatigue from a recent hard week), your body is telling you something.

**Sleep disruption despite tiredness.** Counterintuitively, chronic overtraining often causes sleep disruption — difficulty falling asleep, frequent waking, or waking unrefreshed — even when you're exhausted. This is a hormonal disruption signal (cortisol dysregulation).

**Mood changes.** Increased irritability, loss of motivation for running, difficulty concentrating, and mild depression are documented symptoms of overtraining syndrome. If running feels like a chore every day for two weeks, something is wrong.

**Frequent illness.** Getting colds or minor infections more frequently than usual indicates suppressed immune function — a hallmark of chronic overtraining.

## The Most Common Causes for Ultra Runners

**Ramping volume too quickly.** The standard guidance is no more than 10% weekly volume increase. Many beginner ultra runners, excited about their race goal, add 20–30% per week in early training and arrive at peak training already depleted.

**Inadequate sleep.** Training stress plus life stress plus inadequate sleep is the overtraining formula. You can train at high volume if you sleep 8+ hours. You cannot train at high volume on 5–6 hours of sleep without consequences.

**Not eating enough.** Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) — not eating enough to support your training load — is widespread in running communities and mimics overtraining symptoms.

**Ignoring minor injuries.** Running through small injuries adds stress while reducing recovery capacity. Minor injuries that persist are a sign your recovery capacity is already overwhelmed.

## What to Do When You're Overtrained

**Reduce volume immediately.** Cut by 40–50% for 1–2 weeks. This is not weakness — it is a clinical intervention.

**Prioritize sleep.** This is the most important recovery lever. More sleep than you think you need.

**Eat more.** Particularly carbohydrates and protein. Under-fueling during overtraining recovery is the most common mistake.

**Remove other stressors where possible.** Training stress + life stress = total load. Reducing life stress temporarily accelerates training recovery.

**Do not push through.** The instinct to train more to "get back on track" is exactly wrong. More training when overtrained extends recovery time significantly.
