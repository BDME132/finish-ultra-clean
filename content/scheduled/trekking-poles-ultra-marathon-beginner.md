Most first-time ultra runners either don't think about poles until race day or bring them and have no idea how to use them. Poles done correctly save your legs on climbs and can mean the difference between finishing strong and limping in. Here's everything you need to know.

## Do You Actually Need Trekking Poles?

Poles are not necessary for every ultra. For most beginner 50Ks on moderate terrain (under 3,000 feet of elevation gain), you don't need them.

Poles become genuinely valuable when: your race has 5,000+ feet of elevation gain, the course includes sustained steep climbs over 30 minutes, or you have a history of knee problems on descents.

If in doubt, check the race course profile and read past-runner reports on UltraSignup. If the reviews mention "hiking the climbs" or "technical descents," poles will help.

## How to Use Poles on Uphills

On a sustained uphill, poles should work in a natural cross-body rhythm: **plant your right pole as your left foot strikes, plant your left pole as your right foot strikes**. This mirrors your natural arm swing and creates a four-point propulsion system.

Push through the pole with your arm and shoulder, not just your wrist. Think of pressing the handle down toward the ground and behind you — this is where the power comes from. Done correctly, poles shift 20–30% of uphill effort from your legs to your upper body, which significantly delays quad fatigue.

Your pole tip should plant close to your body — not far out in front — and you should be pushing back, not pushing down.

## How to Use Poles on Descents

Poles on descents are primarily for **balance and stability**, not braking. Plant them to the side for stability on slippery sections, rocky terrain, or stream crossings. Don't lean on them heavily or use them as brakes — that puts your wrists at injury risk.

On runnable descents, stow poles on your vest and let your arms swing freely for balance.

## How to Stow Poles While Running

This is the skill most beginners skip practicing before race day. Collapsible poles need to be folded and attached to your vest while you're moving — fumbling with poles on a flat runnable section wastes time and is frustrating.

Practice at home: fold the poles, tuck them under the vest bungee cords or into the dedicated pole loops, while wearing your loaded vest. Do this ten times before race day until it's automatic.

## The Best Poles for Ultra Running

**[Black Diamond Distance Z](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Black+Diamond+Distance+Z+Trekking+Poles&tag=finishultra-20) — $120–$130**
The most popular ultralight folding pole in ultra running. Folds into three sections, collapses to 13 inches, weighs 9.4oz per pole (pair is under 19oz). Stows cleanly on most running vests.

**[Leki Micro Trail Pro](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Leki+Micro+Trail+Pro&tag=finishultra-20) — $150–$170**
Premium alternative with a better strap system and slightly more durable construction. Preferred by runners who race frequently with poles.

Avoid cheap telescoping poles for ultra running — they don't collapse small enough to stow on a vest, and the weight penalty is significant.

## Practicing With Poles Before Race Day

If you plan to race with poles, train with them. Your arm, shoulder, and core muscles need to adapt to the extra work. Start incorporating poles into your long runs 6–8 weeks before race day.

Run at least two of your long runs entirely with poles. This builds the specific muscles used in poling and — more importantly — teaches you how to manage poles at aid stations, on technical terrain, and during hydration stops.
