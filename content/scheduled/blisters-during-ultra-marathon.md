Blisters are the most common non-fitness reason that ultra runners slow down or drop from races. They're also almost entirely preventable with the right prep — and manageable mid-race if you know what to do.

## Why Ultra Runners Get Blisters

Blisters form when friction creates a separation between layers of skin, which then fills with fluid. In ultras, several factors amplify normal blister risk: **time** (more miles = more friction accumulation), **moisture** (sweat, stream crossings, rain soften skin and increase friction coefficient), **heat** (foot swelling increases pressure in shoes), and **terrain change** (different surfaces create different friction patterns).

The most common blister sites: heel, ball of foot, big toe sides, between toes (2nd/3rd toe junction), and under the balls of the feet.

## How to Prevent Blisters Before Race Day

**Socks:** [Injinji Trail Midweight Crew](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Injinji+Trail+Midweight+Crew&tag=finishultra-20) toe socks eliminate between-toe blisters by creating a physical barrier between each toe. This solves 40–50% of ultra blister problems instantly.

**Foot treatment:** Apply [Trail Toes](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Trail+Toes+Ultra&tag=finishultra-20) or petroleum jelly to your entire foot before the race — heel, ball, toes, between toes. Reapply at aid stations where you have drop bag access.

**Shoe fit:** Your race shoes should be a half size larger than your normal shoes. Feet swell during long runs. Shoes that fit perfectly in the store create pressure points at mile 25 when your feet are swollen.

**Pre-taping hot spots:** If you have a chronic blister location from training, pre-tape it before the race with Leukotape. Don't wait for it to develop — prevent it.

## How to Treat a Blister Mid-Race

The moment you notice a hot spot — not a blister yet, just an uncomfortable friction point — is the right time to stop at the next aid station. Waiting until a hot spot becomes a painful blister takes a 3-minute fix and makes it a 10-minute fix.

**At the aid station:**
1. Sit down and remove your shoe and sock
2. Clean the area with an alcohol wipe if available
3. If it's a hot spot (no blister yet): apply Trail Toes or petroleum jelly and cover with Leukotape
4. If it's an intact blister: leave it intact, apply jelly, cover with Leukotape
5. If it's a large painful blister: drain it by piercing the edge (not center) with a sterile needle, leave the roof intact, apply antibiotic ointment, cover with Leukotape
6. Put on a dry sock if available, replace shoe

**The key:** Never remove the blister roof entirely. The skin underneath is raw and tender. The roof provides protection that lets you continue running. A drained, taped blister with the roof intact is manageable for many more miles.

## What to Put in Your Drop Bag for Blisters

Every drop bag should contain:
- [Leukotape P](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Leukotape+P&tag=finishultra-20) ($10–$15) — the gold standard blister tape
- Sterile lancets or safety pins (for draining)
- Alcohol wipes
- Antibiotic ointment (Neosporin or equivalent)
- A fresh pair of dry socks
- Trail Toes or petroleum jelly for reapplication

Aid stations at most ultras have basic foot care supplies, but having your own in a drop bag means not depending on whether they've run out.

## What Not to Do

**Don't ignore a hot spot.** The 2-minute stop to apply tape is worth 10 times what the delay costs.

**Don't completely remove the blister roof.** This is the most common mistake. The raw tissue underneath is extremely painful and vulnerable to infection.

**Don't use duct tape.** It doesn't breathe, removes with your skin, and is not designed for athletic use. Use Leukotape.

**Don't try new socks on race day.** Every sock brand, material, and thickness performs differently on your specific foot. Test everything in training.
