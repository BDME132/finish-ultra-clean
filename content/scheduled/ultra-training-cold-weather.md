Winter is when most recreational runners lose their base. Ultra runners who train through winter arrive at spring races significantly stronger than those who took 3 months off. Cold weather training is not comfortable. It is worth it.

## How Should You Dress for Cold Weather Trail Running?

The rule for cold weather running is to **dress as if it's 15 degrees warmer than it is.** You should feel slightly cool at the start. If you're comfortable standing still in your gear, you're overdressed and you'll be overheating within a mile.

The three-layer system works:

**Base layer:** Moisture-wicking synthetic or merino wool. Never cotton. The base layer moves sweat away from your skin. A wet cotton base layer in cold weather is dangerous.

**Mid layer:** Lightweight fleece or insulated vest for temperatures below 25°F. Skip this layer in temperatures above 35°F if you run hard.

**Outer layer:** Wind-resistant and water-resistant. It doesn't need to be fully waterproof — fully waterproof shells trap sweat. A soft-shell jacket that blocks wind and light rain is better for running than a hard-shell.

**Extremities first:** Your hands and ears lose heat faster than your core. A light pair of running gloves and a thin wool hat weigh almost nothing and make a 20-degree run feel manageable. Once you're warm you can stuff gloves in your vest pocket.

## How Do You Manage Footing on Icy or Snowy Trails?

Ice is the most dangerous part of winter trail running, not cold temperatures.

**Yaktrax or Kahtoola MicroSpikes** are traction devices that strap over your existing trail shoes and provide grip on packed snow and ice. Yaktrax work well on hardpack; MicroSpikes are more aggressive for icy technical terrain.

[Shop Kahtoola MicroSpikes on Amazon →](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Kahtoola+MicroSpikes&tag=finishultra-20)

Alternatively, run with a slightly shorter stride on slippery terrain — more foot strikes per minute means less time committed to each step, which reduces slipping. Slow down on descents. Ice on a downhill trail can end your training season in one step.

## Should You Adjust Your Pacing in Cold Weather?

Yes. Run by effort, not pace. Cold air is denser and harder to breathe, your body is working to maintain core temperature, and muscles take longer to warm up in cold conditions. Your easy pace in 20°F weather may be 30–60 seconds per mile slower than your easy pace in 60°F weather. That is normal and not a sign of lost fitness.

**Warm up before going hard.** The first 15 minutes of any cold weather run should be easy. Your muscles, tendons, and connective tissue are literally colder and less elastic at the start. Rushing into your target pace in cold weather is how calves get strained and Achilles tendons get irritated.

## How Do You Stay Fueled in Cold Weather?

Cold blunts your sense of thirst, making dehydration sneaky in winter. You still need to drink, even when you don't feel like it.

Nutrition is more critical in cold because your body burns extra calories on thermoregulation. On runs over 75 minutes in temperatures below 30°F, increase your calorie intake by roughly 10% and don't skip electrolytes.

**Practical tip:** Warm fluids are significantly more pleasant in cold weather. Carry [Tailwind Endurance Fuel](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Tailwind+Endurance+Fuel&tag=finishultra-20) in a soft flask inside your vest (body heat keeps it from freezing) rather than in an exposed water bottle.

## What's the Minimum Effective Cold Weather Training Plan?

If you're maintaining a base through winter between races, the minimum that preserves your ultra fitness is:

- 3 runs per week (one long run, two easy runs)
- Long run: at least 12–14 miles, on trails if possible
- Continue back-to-back weekends monthly

Everything beyond that is a bonus. Consistency through winter — even reduced volume — keeps you miles ahead of runners who take the season off.

## When to Move Training Indoors

Use the treadmill when: roads or trails are ice-covered, wind chill is below -10°F, or you're recovering from an illness. Treadmill miles count. See our full breakdown of [treadmill training for ultra runners](/blog/treadmill-ultra-training) for how to replicate trail-specific training indoors.
