Drop bags are confusing the first time. You prepare a bag before the race, hand it over before the start, and it magically appears at an aid station miles into the course. Getting this right means the difference between a well-supported second half and a scramble.

## What Is a Drop Bag?

A drop bag is any bag — duffel, backpack, gallon ziplock, or designated race bag — that you prepare before the race and submit to the race organizers. They transport it to a specific aid station on the course. You access it when you pass through that aid station.

Not all ultras offer drop bags. Most 50Ks do, typically at one or two locations on the course. 50-milers and 100-milers almost always offer drop bags at multiple aid stations. Check your specific race handbook.

## When Do Drop Bags Matter Most?

For beginner 50K runners, the drop bag matters most in three scenarios:

**Race extends into darkness:** If there's any chance you'll be running after sunset, having a headlamp in your drop bag eliminates the stress of carrying it from the start.

**Foot problems develop:** A dry pair of socks and blister treatment supplies can be the difference between finishing and dropping.

**Your nutrition plan changes mid-race:** Your stomach stops tolerating your primary fuel. Having backup nutrition options in your drop bag — broth, real food, a different gel flavor — provides alternatives.

## What to Pack in a 50K Drop Bag

Pack like you're solving for the most likely problems, not every possible problem. A drop bag that's too full wastes your time searching through it.

**Footwear and feet:**
- 1 fresh pair of socks (in a ziplock to keep dry)
- Blister supplies: Leukotape, sterile lancets, antibiotic ointment
- [Trail Toes](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Trail+Toes+Ultra&tag=finishultra-20) for foot reapplication

**Nutrition backup:**
- 2–3 gels or real food items (bananas, dates, rice cakes)
- Small bag of pretzels or salty snacks
- If there's no broth at the aid station — instant broth packets

**Gear:**
- Headlamp with fresh batteries (if race might go to darkness)
- Light gloves and ear covering (for cold weather or night)
- Anti-chafe stick ([Body Glide travel size](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Body+Glide+travel+size&tag=finishultra-20))
- Small flashlight backup

**Comfort:**
- Wipes for a quick face/hands refresh
- Ibuprofen or your chosen pain relief (know your race's rules on aid station medication)
- A short note to yourself ("You're doing great. Eat something. Keep moving.")

## How to Label Your Drop Bag

Write on multiple sides with a large permanent marker:
- **Your full name**
- **Your bib number**
- **The aid station name** (exactly as printed in the race handbook)

Most race handbooks specify labeling requirements — follow them exactly. Unlabeled or incorrectly labeled bags can be missed.

Attach the label with duct tape if using a soft bag. Put a copy of the label inside the bag in case the outside becomes illegible.

## How to Use a Drop Bag Efficiently

Stop. Don't stand and rummage. Pull the bag to a sitting area. Have a clear mental list of what you need. Grab those items and nothing else. Be out in under 5 minutes.

The most common drop bag mistake: spending 15 minutes reorganizing everything, sitting down, and getting stiff. Know what you're getting before you arrive. Practice this in your head during training: "At the drop bag I need: socks, headlamp, two gels."

## Packing the Morning-Of

Do not pack your drop bag the night before a race where morning conditions might change. Weather changes, your feet feel different, you decide to add a warm layer. Pack 80% the night before and finalize the morning of based on actual conditions.
