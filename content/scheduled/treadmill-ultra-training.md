Treadmill miles are not inferior miles. They're just different. Here's how to use the treadmill strategically as an ultra runner and where it falls short.

## What Can a Treadmill Do for Ultra Running Training?

A treadmill is excellent for **building aerobic base**, **practicing nutrition timing** on long runs, **running in bad weather without injury risk**, and **hitting specific effort levels** with precise pace and incline control.

For base building — the majority of ultra training — treadmill miles are nearly equivalent to outdoor road miles. The cardiovascular adaptation, muscular development, and caloric burn are all comparable.

## What Can't a Treadmill Do?

The treadmill cannot replicate **trail-specific movement patterns**: the lateral ankle engagement from uneven terrain, the braking forces on steep technical descents, the focus required to navigate roots and rocks.

If you're training for a technical mountain 50K with 8,000 feet of climbing, a treadmill is a supplement, not a substitute. You need trail time for the specific demands of your race. If you're training for a moderate 50K on groomed trails, treadmill training covers a much larger portion of your needs.

## How to Make Treadmill Miles Count for Ultra Training

**Always use incline.** Set a minimum of 1–1.5% to account for the absence of wind resistance. For training runs, 2–3% incline is a good default that better simulates real-world effort.

**Use incline intervals for hill training.** A treadmill hill workout can genuinely replicate climbing effort. Try: 10 minutes warm-up at 1%, then 6 x [4 minutes at 8–10% incline at easy effort / 2 minutes at 1% recovery]. This builds the hip flexor and glute strength specific to uphill running.

**Practice nutrition during long treadmill runs.** Set the treadmill to a comfortable easy pace and run 2+ hours. This is the ideal environment to practice eating while running — no trail navigation required, just open your [Tailwind](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Tailwind+Endurance+Fuel&tag=finishultra-20) and practice sipping every 20 minutes. Time how long it takes to eat a gel without slowing down. By race day, these mechanics should be automatic.

**Run longer than you think you need to.** Treadmill runs feel longer psychologically than outdoor runs. A 15-mile treadmill run is genuinely hard. Use podcasts, playlists, or race footage on a screen in front of you. Breaking the run into 5-mile mental segments helps.

## What Does a Good Treadmill Ultra Training Week Look Like?

For a runner training through winter with 3–4 treadmill days and 1–2 outdoor days available:

**Monday:** Rest or strength work (20 minutes — squats, lunges, calf raises)
**Tuesday:** Treadmill easy run, 6–8 miles, 2% incline
**Wednesday:** Treadmill hill intervals, 8–10 miles total
**Thursday:** Rest
**Friday:** Treadmill easy run, 6 miles, 1% incline
**Saturday:** Outdoor long run on trail, whatever conditions allow (12–20 miles)
**Sunday:** Easy outdoor run or treadmill recovery, 5–6 miles

This structure keeps trail-specific work on the weekend while treadmill days build the aerobic volume that drives ultra fitness.

## When Is a Treadmill Better Than Running Outside?

Use the treadmill instead of outdoors when: trails are icy and a fall would set back your training, wind chill is below -10°F, air quality is poor (wildfire smoke, high pollution), or you're recovering from a minor illness and need controlled conditions.

The treadmill is not an excuse to avoid uncomfortable weather — that's valuable training. It's a tool for conditions where outdoor running poses actual risk.

## Strength Work to Complement Treadmill Training

The biggest gap from treadmill-heavy training is the reduced ankle and hip stability work from flat, predictable surfaces. Fill this gap with targeted strength work.
