Caffeine is the most researched, most effective, and most universally used performance enhancer in endurance sports. Used correctly in an ultra, it can be the difference between a strong finish and a death march. Used incorrectly, it accelerates GI problems and makes a bad day worse.

## What Does Caffeine Actually Do During an Ultra?

Caffeine works through two primary mechanisms during endurance exercise:

**Reduces perceived effort.** By blocking adenosine receptors in the brain, caffeine makes a given pace feel easier. You're working just as hard — it just doesn't feel that way. This is especially valuable in the back half of a race when effort perception climbs regardless of actual output.

**Increases fat oxidation.** Caffeine shifts your fuel mix slightly toward fat burning, sparing glycogen for when you need it most. The effect is modest but measurable over 6+ hours of racing.

A 2023 meta-analysis in the British Journal of Sports Medicine confirmed caffeine improves endurance performance by an average of 2–4% — meaningful over the 7-hour window of a typical 50K.

## When Should You Use Caffeine in an Ultra?

The most common mistake is using caffeine too early. If you start with a caffeinated gel at mile 5, you've burned your caffeine window when you need it most — after mile 20.

**Best timing for a 50K (6–9 hours):**
- Miles 0–15: No caffeine. Run on glycogen and race-day adrenaline.
- Miles 15–25: Optional first caffeine dose — 1 caffeinated gel or 2 caffeine chews.
- Miles 25–finish: Second caffeine dose if needed for the push to the finish.

**For night sections (50-miler and beyond):** Begin caffeine supplementation 30–60 minutes before darkness. Alertness and reaction time decline significantly after midnight even in well-rested runners. This is when caffeine is most valuable.

## How Much Caffeine Is Effective?

The research-supported effective dose is **3–6mg per kilogram of body weight**:

| Body weight | Effective dose range |
|---|---|
| 130 lbs (59 kg) | 175–355mg |
| 150 lbs (68 kg) | 200–410mg |
| 170 lbs (77 kg) | 230–460mg |
| 190 lbs (86 kg) | 260–515mg |

Spread this over 2–3 doses across your race. Don't take the full dose at once.

## What to Watch Out For

**Caffeine withdrawal mid-race.** If you're a heavy daily coffee drinker (4+ cups/day), caffeine withdrawal can cause headaches starting around hour 8–12. Either maintain some caffeine intake throughout or do a 5-day pre-race reduction to reset your tolerance. Don't cold-turkey quit the week before a race.

**GI acceleration.** Caffeine speeds up gut motility. For many runners this is a non-issue. For runners already managing GI sensitivity, test your caffeine strategy on long training runs first — especially if you plan to use it after mile 20 when your gut is already under stress.

**Too much at once.** 400mg+ in a single dose causes jitteriness, elevated heart rate, and anxiety — all counterproductive during a race. Spread doses over 60+ minute intervals.

Test everything in training. Caffeine strategy, like nutrition strategy, should have zero unknowns by race day.
