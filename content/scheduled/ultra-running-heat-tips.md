Heat is the most common race-day variable that destroys first-time ultra runners. You can train perfectly for 16 weeks and have your race unravel in the first three hot miles because you underestimated the conditions. Here's how to handle it.

## What Heat Does to Your Body During a Run

When your core temperature rises, your body redirects blood flow to your skin for cooling, away from your working muscles. Your heart rate increases at the same perceived effort. Your ability to process nutrition declines. Decision-making degrades. And if your core temperature reaches 104°F (40°C), you enter dangerous heat illness territory.

The compounding factor in ultras: dehydration accumulates over hours. A runner who loses 2% of body weight in water sees a measurable performance decline. By 5%, serious cognitive and physical impairment sets in.

## How to Adjust Your Pace in Heat

A practical rule: **slow your goal pace by 30–60 seconds per mile for every 10°F above your normal training temperature.** If you train in 55°F weather and race in 85°F weather, add 90–180 seconds per mile to your target pace.

This isn't optional — it's physiology. The runners who try to hit their cool-weather goal pace in 90°F heat are the ones who end up in the medical tent at mile 18.

## Pre-Race Heat Preparation

**Heat acclimatization (2 weeks before race):** Run at the hottest part of the day for 10–14 consecutive days. Your body responds by increasing plasma volume, improving sweat efficiency, and lowering your core temperature at a given exercise intensity. This is the single most effective preparation for a hot race.

**Pre-hydrate:** In the 24–48 hours before a hot race, drink significantly more than usual and add electrolytes to your fluids. Start the race with full glycogen and full hydration.

**Check the weather report:** Know the forecast in detail — not just high temperature but humidity, cloud cover, and whether it will be hotter in the early miles or later. Adjust your strategy accordingly.

## Race Day Heat Management

**Use ice at every aid station.** Fill your hat, put ice cubes in your vest chest pockets against your neck, dump cold water on your head and forearms. Your body has large blood vessels in your wrists and neck — cooling these cools your core quickly.

**Slow on climbs, more than usual.** In heat, climbing effort spikes your core temperature faster than on a cool day. Walk more aggressively in hot conditions.

**Electrolytes are more important, not less.** Hot weather means higher sweat rate means more sodium loss. Use [LMNT](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=LMNT+Electrolyte&tag=finishultra-20) (1,000mg sodium per packet) or [SaltStick capsules](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=SaltStick+Caps&tag=finishultra-20) in addition to your normal [Tailwind](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=Tailwind+Endurance+Fuel&tag=finishultra-20) nutrition.

**Drink to thirst, not to a schedule.** In cool conditions, scheduled drinking is fine. In heat, your thirst mechanism is a better guide to your actual fluid needs. Drink when thirsty, always with electrolytes.

## Warning Signs of Heat Illness

Know these. React to them immediately.

**Heat exhaustion (stop and cool down):** heavy sweating, pale skin, rapid weak pulse, nausea, muscle cramps, headache, dizziness.

**Heat stroke (medical emergency):** confusion or slurred speech, no sweating despite heat, very high body temperature, loss of consciousness. Get to medical staff immediately.

If you notice any sign of heat stroke in yourself or another runner, stop running, lie in shade, and send for help. This is a life-threatening situation.

## The Mindset Shift for Hot Race Days

Accept early that your finish time goal changes in heat. A time goal set in training in cool conditions is not relevant on a 90°F race day. Shift your focus from pace to completion, from splits to survival management. Finishing a hot ultra in a slower time is a far better outcome than a DNF or a medical situation.
