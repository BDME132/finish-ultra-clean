"Just finish" is what every first-time ultra runner says they want. It sounds humble. It sounds smart. But it's also so vague that it gives you nothing to train toward and no way to know if your preparation is working.

Here's how to set a better goal.

## Why Vague Goals Make Training Harder

A vague goal creates a vague training plan. "Just finish" doesn't tell you how long to make your long runs, what pace to train at, or how to evaluate whether a training week was successful. You end up doing random work and hoping for the best.

Clear goals — even modest ones — create structure. Structure drives consistency. Consistency wins ultras.

## How to Set a Process Goal That Actually Works

A **process goal** tracks your behavior, not your race result. It is completely within your control regardless of weather, terrain, or how you feel on race day.

Good process goals for first-time ultra runners:
- Complete every scheduled long run without cutting it short
- Practice nutrition on every run over 90 minutes
- Run on trail at least 2 days per week during the training block
- Complete two night runs before race day
- Do strength work twice a week for 8 consecutive weeks

These goals give you something to evaluate weekly. They build the specific habits that translate to finishing an ultra.

## How to Set a Realistic Outcome Goal

An **outcome goal** is your target finish time or result. To set a useful one, you need two things: historical race data and honest self-assessment.

**Step 1:** Go to UltraSignup.com, find your target race, and look at past results. What is the median finishing time? What is the back-of-the-pack time? This tells you the realistic range.

**Step 2:** Honestly assess your current fitness. Can you run 15 miles comfortably? Have you run beyond 20 miles in training? Are you a 4:30 marathon runner or a 5:30 runner?

**Step 3:** Set a 3-tier goal:
- **A goal:** Your best realistic scenario — everything goes right
- **B goal:** A solid day — a few rough patches but you execute your plan
- **C goal:** Your safety net — you finish no matter what

Example for a 50K on moderate terrain with 3,000 feet of elevation:
- **A goal:** Under 7 hours
- **B goal:** 7–8 hours
- **C goal:** Before the cutoff (whatever that is)

## What to Write Down Before Training Starts

On a note in your phone or a piece of paper, write:
1. Your race name and date
2. One process goal you will do every week
3. Your A/B/C finish time goals
4. Why this race matters to you (one specific sentence)

The "why" is the one you'll call up at mile 20 when everything hurts. "Vague desire to see if I can do it" fades under real suffering. "Proving to my kids that hard things are possible" does not.

## Adjusting Goals on Race Day

Race day will not match your training runs. Conditions change. Your legs may feel flat from taper. The terrain may be harder than you expected. Adjust your goals based on the first 10 miles of actual experience — don't hold rigidly to your A goal if your body is telling you something different by mile 8.

The single most important rule: finish. A B-goal finish teaches you more than a DNF while chasing an A goal. The data from your first ultra — what worked, what didn't, where you slowed, what you ate — is priceless for every race that follows.
