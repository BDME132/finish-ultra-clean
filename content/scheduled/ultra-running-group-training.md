The solo long run is a necessary part of ultra training. But runners who train entirely alone have lower completion rates, more training inconsistency, and worse race experiences than those embedded in a running community. Here's how to find your people.

## Why Community Makes Ultra Training Better

Ultra training is demanding enough that most runners drop their training when motivation is the only force sustaining them. External accountability — other people expecting you at the trailhead — is a significantly more reliable force than internal motivation, which fluctuates with sleep, stress, and life circumstances.

Beyond accountability, experienced runners in your community provide practical knowledge that takes years to accumulate alone: which trails to use for which training phases, what gear actually works, which local races are appropriate for first-timers, and what to do when something goes wrong in training.

## How to Find a Trail Running Group

**Local running store:** This is the fastest path. Independent running stores almost universally host group runs and can connect you directly with the trail running community. Walk in, introduce yourself as someone training for their first 50K, and ask who else is doing the same.

**Facebook groups:** Search "[your city] trail running" or "[your region] ultra running." Active Facebook groups in this category exist in most metro areas and many smaller cities. Post your current training status and ask about group runs.

**Meetup.com:** Many trail running groups organize through Meetup. Search "trail running" in your area and filter by upcoming events.

**Strava clubs:** Search Strava for local running clubs. Many active clubs post group run announcements and have members you can connect with.

**UltraSignup race pages:** Look at the results from local races. The runners near your pace from recent local events are your potential training partners. Strava often links to race finisher profiles.

## What to Expect at Your First Group Run

Trail running groups are almost universally welcoming to new runners. Pace anxiety is the most common barrier — new runners worry they'll be too slow and hold the group back.

Most trail running groups use a "no-drop" policy: nobody gets left behind. Faster runners circle back or wait at trail junctions. If you're nervous, email the organizer before showing up and introduce yourself. This is completely normal and the response will almost always be enthusiastic.

Bring your own water and nutrition. Introduce yourself. Ask questions — experienced trail runners love talking about trails, gear, and races. You'll learn more in one group long run than from hours of reading online.

## Building Your Own Group If Nothing Exists

If you live in an area without an active trail running community, create one. The barrier is lower than it seems.

**Step 1:** Post in three places: your local running store's social media, a local Facebook group, and a trail-specific platform like AllTrails.

**Step 2:** Propose a specific time, place, and length. "Saturday 8am at [trailhead], easy 8-10 miles, all paces welcome, no drop." Specific beats vague.

**Step 3:** Show up. Even if only one or two people come, that's the group. Groups tend to grow through word of mouth once they have a consistent schedule.

The ultra running community is disproportionately full of people who want to share the experience. Most groups started because one person posted an invitation.

## Pairing Group Runs With Solo Training

Not every training run needs to be social. Use group runs for: weekend long runs (safety, accountability, conversation), out-and-back routes where you can adjust distance to match the group, and race recaps and gear discussions.

Do solo runs for: early morning weekday runs, specific training sessions where you need to follow a precise effort or schedule, and the mental training of solitary sustained effort that ultras require.
